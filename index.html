A Calligraphic Journey

By: Maryam Biazaran - May 2019

I came to the US in summer 2013 to study for a PhD degree in business. Although I didn't get a PhD degree at the end, I'm grateful for the 4-year self-discovery journey that I went through during my tenure as a PhD student.

The course load in the first two years was enough to keep me busy and away from any other activities. Once I was done with courses, I had to study for the qualifying exam. I discovered my inner-calligrapher during the 8-week study-period leading up to the exam.

Here I share highlights of this journey. My Instagram portfolio came in handy in building this page. Hope you enjoy!

Distraction

May 2015 - I have been studying for two weeks. Graduation in over. School is quiet. Faculty are wrapping up and getting ready for summer break. First-years are already gone. Third-year and above are dealing with their more complicated life.
What is more interesting than studying? ANYTHING!

The struggle was real. So... things started coming out... slowly!

I found this soothing effect in using a pen for something other than proving theorems or grading undergraduate or MBA case studies and exams.

Hmmm... What else can I do?

Mentor

September 2015 - While Instagram and Pinterest of full of inspiring content, self-teaching can be sometimes frustrating. Thanks to Google, I found the St. Louis Calligraphy Guild and learned about local classes.

I signed up for the first available class and Cheryl Tefft became my mentor. I studied with Cheryl for about 2 years. She is a hardworking and extremely talented artist. Hand-lettering is her full-time job and she's very good at it.

Uncial

Wikipedia says:

Uncial is a majuscule script (written entirely in capital letters) commonly used from the 4th to 8th centuries AD by Latin and Greek scribes. Uncial letters were used to write Greek, Latin, and Gothic.

Uncial is a strange lettering style. However, it is the base for more fancy lettering styles like "Italic". Here is my mono-line practice of a few alphabet sentences in Uncial, together with English alphabet written with Pilot parallel pen.

Italic

As mentioned above, Uncial is the base for Italic lettering. Essentially, Italic is "Uncial written on a slant".

Mastering Dip-Pen

Before this course, I'd been using cartridge pens (like Pilot parallel pens) in which ink is stored in a plastic container that feeds the nib. Now, it was time to grow up and use real ink. This is still limited to broad nib (broad-edge) pens. Working with "Pointed-pen" is still a dream at this point.

Here is a few extra challenges we had to face in this course:

  • Ink - good ink for calligraphy needs to have a certain consistency. There are different types of ink in the market. Certain chemicals are used to help reach the ideal consistency. This is a good discussion on varieties of inks used for calligraphy.

  • Nib - different nibs are designed to get different jobs done. Choice of nib sometimes depends on the paper. Some nibs have reservoirs and can hold more ink so less 'loading' is required. Complicated stuff!

Turns out, working with real ink is hard and it can be very messy! That's why paper towels are a calligrapher's best friend.

My Favorite real ink is walnut ink. It has a nice texture and you can choose adjust the intensity to get your desired shade of brown. Of course I used walnut ink for my final project of "mastering dip-pen" class.

Copperplate Roundhand

Learning to write in Copperplate is every beginner's dream. The long journey of mastering basics was worth it. Copperplate is magical but complicated and requires lots and lots of practice. On top of the skills that we had mastered so far, we had to learn how to work with an oblique pen and a pointed nib!

In every course, we had a "Single Quote" project that involved a clean finish of a sentence or quote, using the techniques we learned in the courses. Extra challenges are always fun. Writing on a circle is not hard, but can be tricky. I gave it a shot.
